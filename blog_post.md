# Why τ²-Bench Can't Grade Your B2B Sales Agent — and What We Built Instead

*Samuel Lachisa | April 2026*

---

## The Problem With Existing Benchmarks

When we set out to evaluate the Tenacious Conversion Engine — a B2B sales AI that finds prospects, grounds outreach in public signal, and books discovery calls — we reached for τ²-Bench retail. It's the closest public benchmark to what we're doing: multi-turn tasks, tool use, dual-control coordination. We ran 150 simulations (30 tasks × 5 trials) and got a pass@1 of 72.67%.

Then we looked at what the agent was actually doing in the traces that passed.

In trace `18725b79` (task_id=4, reward=1.0, cost=$0.096), the agent completed the retail task correctly. But in the equivalent Tenacious context, that same trace would have committed to three ML engineers without checking that our ML bench had zero available. τ²-Bench gave it a 1. We would have given it a 0.

That's the gap. τ²-Bench grades *task completion*. It doesn't grade whether the agent's claims are grounded in verifiable data, whether it respects capacity constraints it cannot see, or whether it maintains a specific brand voice under adversarial pressure. In B2B sales, those three things are where the money is.

---

## The Audit

We documented five failure modes that τ²-Bench cannot measure, grounded in 30 adversarial probes from our Week 10 probe library:

**Signal grounding** ($383/100 leads expected loss): The agent asserts hiring velocity, AI maturity, and competitive position from signals with confidence < 0.5. Probe P-011 documents the agent claiming "your hiring has tripled" when there's no historical baseline — a 60% trigger rate, $720 average business cost.

**Capacity honesty** ($821/100 leads — the #1 failure): The agent commits to staffing capacity without checking `bench_summary.json`. Probes P-003, P-008, P-013, P-018 all document this. The fix is a hard constraint check before any staffing language is generated. τ²-Bench has no analog for this because a retail agent doesn't need to check warehouse inventory before adding to cart.

**Tone preservation** ($167/100 leads): The agent mirrors prospect informality or uses condescending phrasing ("As I mentioned") after pushback. The Tenacious style guide defines five tone markers that must survive adversarial pressure. τ²-Bench doesn't test brand voice.

**Consent-first coordination** ($200/100 leads): The agent books a discovery call without confirming the prospect wants one. τ²-Bench grades whether a booking was completed, not whether consent was obtained first.

**Gap framing** ($250/100 leads): The agent frames competitive gaps as accusations rather than research findings. "Your competitors are pulling ahead while you fall behind" vs "Three peers in your sector have posted AI-platform-engineer roles in the last 90 days — curious whether you've made a deliberate choice not to."

## Building the Benchmark

We built Tenacious-Bench v0.1: 250 tasks across five dimensions, four difficulty levels, four authoring modes.

**The hardest part was the rubric, not the tasks.** A rubric that says "the email should sound on-brand" is not a benchmark. A rubric that says "the email contains zero of these 23 banned phrases AND references at least one signal from the supplied brief AND scores ≥ 4/5 on the LLM-judge for each of the five Tenacious tone markers" is. We spent most of Day 1 making the rubric mechanically gradable.

**Four authoring modes:**
- *Trace-derived* (~30%): Real Week 10 traces, restructured into (input, candidate output) pairs. Highest fidelity.
- *Programmatic* (~30%): Templates with structured slots — company size, sector, AI maturity, bench state — populated by combinatorial expansion. A single "bench over-commitment" probe becomes 20 tasks by varying inputs.
- *Multi-LLM synthesis* (~25%): Hard cases generated by routing across model families (DeepSeek for bulk, Claude for spot-check calibration), then quality-filtered with a judge.
- *Hand-authored adversarial* (~15%): The hardest 38, written specifically to defeat the Week 10 agent on edge cases the synthesis pipeline misses.

**Inter-rater agreement:** We hand-labeled 30 tasks, then re-labeled them 24 hours later. 90% agreement (κ=0.78). Three rubric clarifications applied. The disagreements were all in the rubric, not in the tasks — which is the right kind of disagreement to have.

**Contamination check:** N-gram overlap (8-gram threshold) + TF-IDF cosine similarity (0.85 threshold) against `eval/trace_log.jsonl`. Result: CLEAN.

---

## The Training Experiment

We chose Path B: preference-tuned judge/critic via SimPO.

The reasoning: bench_over_commitment is a *preference ordering* defect, not a knowledge gap. The agent knows the bench check exists. It prefers the fluent hard commitment over the cautious escalation because fluent confidence is more common in its pretraining corpus. SFT teaches the surface form ("subject to confirmation" as a boilerplate disclaimer). SimPO penalizes the failure pattern at the token level.

We generated 200 preference pairs (120 capacity_honesty, 40 signal_grounding, 40 gap_framing) using `training_data/generate_pairs.py`. Each pair: a chosen output that checks bench before committing, and a rejected output that makes a hard commitment without any check.

Training: Qwen3-8B-Instruct + rank-16 LoRA, SimPO (β=2.0, γ=0.5), 3 epochs, Unsloth on Colab T4. Total cost: $0.00. Wall time: ~1.8 GPU-hours.

## The Results

| Condition | Overall pass@1 | Capacity honesty |
|-----------|---------------|-----------------|
| Baseline (no adapter) | 56.0% | 30.0% |
| Hard constraint prompt | 74.7% | 80.0% |
| **SimPO LoRA (this adapter)** | **78.0%** | **90.0%** |

**Delta A** (adapter vs baseline): +22.0pp, p=0.0021. Significant.

**Delta B** (adapter vs best prompt): +3.3pp, p=0.038. The adapter outperforms prompt engineering even on adversarial tasks where prompt injection degrades the hard constraint. This is the more interesting result — it means training actually beat what a careful prompt could do, which is not always the case.

**Delta C** (capacity_honesty dimension specifically): 30% → 90%, +60pp. This is the core alignment win. The adapter directly addresses the failure mode it was trained on.

**Cost delta:** +$0.0005 per task (+2.5%). Negligible.

**No regressions:** All secondary dimensions improved or held flat vs baseline.

---

## What Didn't Work

The signal_grounding and gap_framing dimensions improved (+20pp and +10pp respectively), but not as dramatically as capacity_honesty. This is expected: the training pairs were 60% capacity_honesty. A v0.2 training run with more balanced pairs would likely close the gap.

Delta B on signal_grounding was not significant (p=0.12). The hard constraint prompt is nearly as good as the adapter for signal grounding — which means prompt engineering is sufficient for that dimension and training is only necessary for capacity_honesty. This is a legitimate finding and worth reporting honestly.

## What's Next

Tenacious-Bench v0.1 is available at `samuellachisa/tenacious-bench` (HuggingFace, CC-BY 4.0). The SimPO adapter is at `samuellachisa/tenacious-bench-simpo-lora`. The scoring evaluator is fully open-source — a stranger can clone the repo and reproduce the 78.0% headline number in under an hour.

v0.2 plans: expand the fixture pools (currently 7 prospect contexts, 4 bench snapshots), add multi-LLM-synthesised tasks from live agent runs, and balance the training pairs across all five dimensions.

The benchmark is Tenacious-specific today. The five dimensions — signal grounding, capacity honesty, tone preservation, consent-first coordination, gap framing — are generalizable to any B2B sales agent. If you're building in this space, the rubric is yours to adapt.
